Background

Do J-Pop and Western Pop favor different chord transition patterns?

This project stemmed from an observation I saw from YouTube music theorists who have noted that J-Pop and anime soundtracks have a distinctly different harmonic feel from Western pop. As David Bennett Piano identifies, the progression IV → V → iii → vi is a signature pattern in Japanese music, functioning as a counterpart to the Western pop staple I → V → vi → IV.

That observation raised a deeper question: is this a systematic difference, or just a handful of famous examples? This project attempts to answer that by extracting chord transitions from 200 songs across both umbrella genres and visualizing the patterns as a network.

David Bennett Piano — Why Does Japanese Music Sound Different?

Western music theory describes harmony through a common vocabulary — tonic, dominant, subdominant — built around the European classical tradition. But what happens when you apply that same framework to J-Pop, a genre that evolved in Japan under Western influences and indigenous Japanese musical tradition?

The visualization treats chord progressions as a web of nodes that represent chords. Each arrow is a progression observed in real songs. The result is a kind of harmonic roadmap for each genre. It allows you to see what the most common road maps are by filtering the minimum number of chord transitions to show only the most frequent ones over the specified threshold.

Methodology

How the Data Was Built

1

Dataset

Chord progressions were drawn from the Chordonomicon dataset of 679,807 songs hosted on HuggingFace, with genre tags from Spotify's tagging system. Each song includes raw chord symbols, section markers, and release decade.

2

Genre Filtering

Songs were filtered into two categories: J-Pop/J-Rock (matched on tags like j-pop, j-rock, city pop, visual kei, anison) and Eurocentric Western Pop (main_genre = "pop", excluding Asian regional tags). This yielded ~3,000 J-Pop songs and ~73,000 Western Pop songs.

3

Sampling

100 songs per category were selected using decade-stratified random sampling: 20 songs per decade from the 1980s through the 2020s. This was done to ensure representation across a diverse range of musical eras.

4

Key Transposition

All songs were transposed to C major using a Krumhansl-Schmuckler key estimation algorithm, then converted to Roman numeral notation. Minor keys were first shifted to their relative major before transposition, allowing direct harmonic comparison across songs in different keys.

5

Transition Extraction

Consecutive chord pairs were extracted from each song's progression, producing a count of how many times each transition (e.g. I→V) occurred per category per decade. These counts power the network visualization.

Limitations

What to Keep in Mind

Key Detection is Estimated

No key signatures are stored in the dataset. Keys were inferred algorithmically with roughly 70–80% accuracy. Misdetected keys produce transposition errors that may introduce noise into the Roman numeral data.

Genre Tags Are Noisy

Spotify's tags are user-generated and inconsistent. Some songs in the J-Pop bucket are mistagged Western artists, and genre boundaries are inherently fuzzy. A small number of non-Japanese artists appear in the J-Pop sample as a result.

Small Sample Size

100 songs per category is a limited repertoire. Patterns observed here are suggestive rather than statistically conclusive. A larger, more carefully curated sample would be needed to make strong claims.

Chord Notation Inconsistency

Chords in the Chordonomicon were contributed by many different users with varying levels of theoretical experience and precision during analysis. Extended chords, slash chords, and modal borrowings are therefore represented inconsistently across songs.

Western Theoretical Framework

Using Roman numeral notation imposes a Western tonal framework on J-Pop, which sometimes draws on pentatonic, modal, or non-functional harmonic logic. Some nuance is inevitably lost in translation.
